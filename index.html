Psychoacoustics Robustness : Exploring Audio Processing Robustness

Research Project, 2025


Abstract

Intelligent audio systems have been widely applied in various scenarios. However, their core component, the speech understanding algorithm, can be misled by carefully constructed adversarial examples, which are imperceptible to humans but can cause the system to make incorrect or even harmful decisions. Certified defense aims to provide a lower bound on accuracy for deep learning algorithms under a certain perturbation constraint. Nevertheless, existing work primarily focuses on bounding adversarial perturbations under ℓp norm, which fail to accurately reflect human auditory perception. This mismatch allows adversaries to craft imperceptible perturbations that bypass both human detection and norm-based defenses. We propose PORSCHE, a novel certified defense framework for audio understanding algorithms, based on psychoacoustically guided randomized smoothing. We present rigorous theoretical analysis and extensive experiments showing that PORSCHE provides certified lower bounds on model accuracy under human perceptual constraints. In the presence of imperceptible adversarial perturbations, PORSCHE raises the certified accuracy from 3.2% to at least 69.3%. This paper bridges the gap between certified defense and human auditory perception, establishing the relationship between model robustness and noise audibility. Our demo is available at https://psychoacousticsrobustness.github.io/PsychoacousticsRobustness/

Our Pipeline

PORSCHE (psychoacoustic-oriented random smoothing framework) provides a novel approach to defending against adversarial attacks in speech understanding systems. The pipeline incorporates perceptual constraints to ensure robustness while maintaining audio quality within human auditory perception limits.

The workflow of PORSCHE: PORSCHE turns an audio understanding model into a spectral-smoothed one such that the robustness of the model can be certified. It first transforms the input audio into spectrogram via STFT. Then the thresholds in the context of the input audio are calculated. These thresholds determine an anisotropic Gaussian distribution, from which we sample multiple spectral noises and add to the original spectrogram. These noisy spectrograms are transformed back into audios, which are fed into the understanding model to generate multiple classification results. A final label and a certified radius can be calculated from the results.

Compare to Undefended Model

Approximate certified accuracy of PORSCHE on the LibriSpeech test-clean set for the ASV task. The hyperparameter σ controls a robustness/accuracy tradeoff. The dashed black line is an upper bound on the empirical robust accuracy of an undefended classifier with the base classifier's architecture. We observe that smoothing significantly enhances the robustness compared to the undefended baseline.

Specifically, models are trained on VoxCeleb1 and VoxCeleb2, and evaluated on the LibriSpeech test-clean set, which is entirely disjoint from the training data. At a perceptual noise threshold of R = 0.032, the attacker achieves only 3.2% accuracy against the undefended model, demonstrating the effectiveness of the targeted attack. In contrast, thanks to the spectrum smoothing effect of PORSCHE, the certified accuracy under the same perceptual constraint improves significantly to 61.6% (σ=10), 68.7% (σ=20), 69.3% (σ=30), 61.6% (σ=40), and 59.5% (σ=50).

Noise Audibility

We define noise audibility as a key metric for evaluating the perceptual impact of adversarial perturbations. Our audibility measure ensures that any defense mechanism considers human auditory perception limits.

Audibility Formula

Equation 1: Audibility Definition
𝒜(δ, x) = Σm=0NF-1 𝒜m(δ, x), 𝒜m(δ, x) = Σk=0⌊N/2⌋ (p̄δ[m,k] / Ωx[m,k])

This audibility metric is evaluated through our comprehensive user study, which demonstrates the effectiveness of our approach in maintaining perceptual quality while providing certified robustness.

User Study

We design a two-stage survey consisting of a training stage and a testing stage. At the training stage, we provide five pairs of audios, each consisting of one clean audio and one adversarial example. The audio pairs are arranged in descending order of the audibility of the adversarial perturbation, 25∼26, 21∼22, 2-2∼2-1, 2-4∼2-3, 2-5∼2-4. We directly tell users that the first audio is clean and the other is noisy, and expect that at this stage users can feel the auditory sensation of the presence of adversarial perturbation.
